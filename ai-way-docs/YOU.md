## The Lie You Were Told

Somewhere along the way, you were told there are two kinds of people:

- **People who build things**: Engineers. Architects. Scientists. "Smart people."
- **People who use things**: Everyone else. Consumers. Regular folks. *You*, maybe.

This is a lie.

It was never about intelligence. It was about *access*. Access to knowledge. Access to tools. Access to time. Access to the confidence that comes from someone once telling you: "Yeah, you can do that."

For most of human history, that access was gatekept. By guilds. By universities. By corporations. By the accident of birth.

Not anymore.

## The Coffee Table Test

Start small. Start with something you *already* want to do.

A coffee table. A garden. A website for your business. A tool that solves a problem no one else has solved because no one else has your exact problem.

Pick one thing. Just one.

Then do what curious people have always done:
1. Ask: "How do people do this?"
2. Study what works. Discard what doesn't.
3. Try. Fail. Learn. Try again.
4. Finish.

That's it. That's the entire process for building anything humans have ever built. From coffee tables to cathedrals. From apps to airplanes. From poetry to particle accelerators.

The process scales. The confidence compounds.

After you build the coffee table, you'll know you can build furniture.
After you build furniture, you'll know you can build things with your hands.
After you build with your hands, you'll wonder: what *else* can I build?

That question—*what else?*—is the most powerful question a human being can ask.
